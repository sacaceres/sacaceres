The MLB has 30 teams split into 6 divisions of 5 teams each. These 6 divisions are further separated into 2 leagues, the National League (NL), and the American League (AL), with 3 divisions and subsequently 15 teams in each. The current playoff format for the league is a 12 team format, 6 from each league. Each division winner is assured a top 3 seed in their league and then next 3 best teams based on record fill out the remaining playoff seeds. The teams are then arranged into a playoff bracket.

The two division winners with the best record in each league receive a bye in the first round, the Wild-Card Round. The remaining division winner, the 3 seed, plays the 6 seed, the playoff team with the worst record, and the 4 and 5 seeds play each other in best of 3 series. The winner of the the 4 vs 5 series plays the 1 seed in the next round and the winner of the 3 vs 6 series plays the 2 seed. Those match-ups in the Division Series are best of 5, and the winner of those face each other in the ALCS or NLCS Championship series in a best of 7. The AL and NL champs face off in the World Series in a best of 7 and the winner is the MLB champion in that year.

An important thing to note for this playoff format is the home field advantage teams gain by higher seeding. Teams with the higher seed in their match-up receive home field advantage which means in the series they will host more home games than their lower seeded opponent. In the Wild-Card Round, the higher seed gets to play at home all three games. In the Division Round the home teams hosts the first two games, and the last game, hosting 3 of the 5 if all games were played in the series. In the Championship Series and the World Series, games 1, 2, 6 & 7 are played at the higher seeded teams home stadium, and games 3, 4, & 5 at the lower seeds home. The higher seeded team in the World Series, since these teams were not seeded together, is the team with the better regular season record.

Playoffs in Sport are all about promoting competition and seeing who truly is the best team that year. One way that can be interrupted is by pre existing rules that grant worse teams, advantages in the playoffs, giving them a better chance to win the league even if they were worse than other playoff teams in the regular season. In the playoff seeding system the MLB has in place, there are scenarios where this is easily possible. Granting division winners guaranteed highest seeds, irregardless of record, can create a scenario where a division winner can be seeded higher than one or multiple teams with a better regular season record. With the MLB also fixing match-ups in advance, having the 4/5 seed always play the 1 seed instead of rearranging the match-ups based on seed so the 1 seed plays the lowest remaining seed in the playoffs, can also create a scenario where a 4 or 5 seed team, that was better than the 2 or 3 seed in the regular season has to not only play in the Wild-Card Round but also the 1 seed in the next round, giving them a very difficult route to winning the league even though they were a top team in the regular season.

That is the case for the New York Yankees in the most recent 2025 MLB playoffs. They lost the division title to the Toronto Blue Jays on a head-to-head tiebreaker, and so despite having the tied the Blue Jays for best record in the AL, they were the 4 seed in the playoffs and then faced Toronto in the AL Division Series (ALDS) and lost.

For our project we want to see if changing the MLB playoff format would significantly change the predicted outcome of the MLB playoffs. We aim to make the regular season results more impactful in the outcome of the playoffs, giving better teams their deserved chance to win the title that season.

To do this we would seed the playoffs by regular season record, tie-breaker being head to head record, granting guaranteed playoff spots to division winners but not a guaranteed top 3 seed. After the Wild-Card Round we’d then re-arrange the match-ups so the 1 seed faces the lowest remaining seed and the 2 seed faces the highest remaining seed.

We focused our project on the 2025 MLB season.

standings <- games %>%
  mutate(winner = if_else(home_win==1, home_key, away_key)) %>%
  select(home_key, away_key, winner) %>%
  pivot_longer(c(home_key, away_key), values_to = "team_key") %>%
  mutate(win = as.integer(team_key == winner)) %>%
  group_by(team_key) %>%
  summarise(W = sum(win), L = n()-sum(win), GP=n(), Wpct = W/(W+L), .groups="drop") %>%
  left_join(team_map %>% select(team_key, team_id, team, league), by="team_key")

calculate_h2h <- function(team1, team2, games_df) {
  h2h_games <- games_df %>%
    filter((home_key == team1 & away_key == team2) | (home_key == team2 & away_key == team1))
  if(nrow(h2h_games) == 0) return(0.5)
  team1_wins <- h2h_games %>%
    mutate(team1_win = case_when(
      home_key == team1 & home_win == 1 ~ 1,
      away_key == team1 & home_win == 0 ~ 1,
      TRUE ~ 0
    )) %>%
    summarise(wins = sum(team1_win)) %>%
    pull(wins)
  return(team1_wins / nrow(h2h_games))
}

seed_league <- function(league_code){
  league_standings <- standings %>%
    filter(league == league_code)
  initial_order <- league_standings %>%
    arrange(desc(Wpct), desc(W), desc(GP))
  final_order <- initial_order %>%
    mutate(temp_rank = row_number()) %>%
    group_by(Wpct, W, GP) %>%
    mutate(tie_count = n()) %>%
    ungroup()
  tied_groups <- final_order %>%
    filter(tie_count > 1) %>%
    group_by(Wpct, W, GP) %>%
    group_split()
  for(group in tied_groups) {
    tied_teams <- group$team_key
    start_rank <- min(group$temp_rank)
    if(length(tied_teams) > 1) {
      h2h_matrix <- matrix(0, nrow = length(tied_teams), ncol = length(tied_teams))
      
      for(i in 1:length(tied_teams)) {
        for(j in 1:length(tied_teams)) {
          if(i != j) {
            h2h_matrix[i,j] <- calculate_h2h(tied_teams[i], tied_teams[j], games)
          }
        }
      }
      
      h2h_win_pct <- rowMeans(h2h_matrix, na.rm = TRUE)

      tied_order <- order(h2h_win_pct, decreasing = TRUE)
      sorted_teams <- tied_teams[tied_order]

      for(i in seq_along(sorted_teams)) {
        final_order$temp_rank[final_order$team_key == sorted_teams[i]] <- start_rank + i - 1
      }
    }
  }

  final_order %>%
    arrange(temp_rank) %>%
    mutate(seed = row_number()) %>%
    select(league, seed, team_key, team, W, L, Wpct)
}

Next, we created a Bradley-Terry Model to create a latent strength estimate for each MLB team based on their 2025 season results. We created a temporary data frame that tracks the winner of each game, whether they were home or away, and then applies a home team advantage to the teams that won at home while not doing so for the teams that won on the road. Then we fit the model, modeling both team strength and home field advantage, with our reference team of the San Francisco Giants.

We chose San Francisco as the reference team as they were 81-81 in the regular season which means they won 81 games and lost 81 games, and as we saw above, finished 8th in the NL. Based on those parameters we also could have chosen the Texas Rangers as our reference as they also finished 81-81 and were 9th in the AL. Either way both teams were the very definition of average for the 2025 MLB season, so they seemed like a good fit to be our reference for our model.

Looking at the summary results from the model, we see the league seems extremely even on paper. Outside of the Rockies who lost 17 more games than the next worst MLB team, every team’s latent strength is within the range of (-.482, .412) which on it’s own doesn’t tell us much. Checking the standard deviations though, again not including the Rockies, every team’s standard deviation on their latent strength is around .22 which is a large amount to vary when their strength was only as high as .412 to begin with. This tells us that using this Bradley-Terry Model, we believe almost every MLB team to be decently close to each other in latent strength. We still see some differences in strength but when predicting these teams at a neutral site game we would expect the odds to be closer to 50/50 than something drastically in favor of one team.

Importantly though, these MLB playoff games are not neutral site games, and looking at the “at home” coefficient in our summary results we see an estimate of .178 and is statistically significant. This further emphasizes the importance of our project, as our model shows a definitive advantage playing at home, and we are adjusting the seeding to give the better teams, more home games in the playoffs. This aligns with our project goal of having the MLB playoffs redesigned to crown the best MLB team that year.

Looking at the playoff teams, we have a smaller spread from the league best Brewers to the Reds, .412 to .087, as shown below.

These latent strength estimates from our Bradley-Terry Model are actually the log odds of a team to beat any other team. Using this we can actually exponentiate these log odds to calculate the probability that one team beats another if we subtract their exponentiated log odds from each other and account for the home field advantage estimate. We did so with the Dodgers and Blue Jays below, this years World Series teams. We calculate the log odds of the Dodgers beating the Blue Jays at home by subtracting the Blue Jays log odds from the dodgers and then adding the home field advantage in. We do the same but subtract the home field advantage estimate from the equation to get the Dodgers percentage chance of beating the Blue Jays on the road, and then leave off the home field advantage completely when calculating the win percentage chance at a neutral site.

With our model, we predict the Dodgers to beat the Blue Jays 52.2% of the time when at home, 43.32% of the time when at Toronto, and 47.74% of the time at a neutral site. It was a 7 game world series with the final game going to extra innings so it seems accurate our model would predict these teams to be close as well. The biggest factor between them would seem to be having home field advantage.

Exponentiating the home field advantage log odds estimate, we see that a team playing at home increases their odds to win by roughly 4.4%. Thus going from playing at home to on the road against an opponnent would see your predicted chance of winning drop about 8.8%. This is a significant percentage amount, and makes the impact of re-seeding teams and changing who gets more home playoff games versus away playoff games that much more important.

calculate_matchup_probs <- function(team1, team2) {
  lambda1 <- lambda_hat[team1, "ability"]
  lambda2 <- lambda_hat[team2, "ability"]
  
  log_odds_home1 <- lambda1 - lambda2 + lambda0_hat
  log_odds_home2 <- lambda1 - lambda2 - lambda0_hat  
  log_odds_neutral <- lambda1 - lambda2
  
  prob_home1 <- exp(log_odds_home1) / (1 + exp(log_odds_home1))
  prob_home2 <- exp(log_odds_home2) / (1 + exp(log_odds_home2))
  prob_neutral <- exp(log_odds_neutral) / (1 + exp(log_odds_neutral))
  
  cat(sprintf("=== %s vs %s ===\n", team1, team2))
  cat(sprintf("At %s: %.2f%%\n", team1, prob_home1 * 100))
  cat(sprintf("At %s: %.2f%%\n", team2, prob_home2 * 100)) 
  cat(sprintf("At neutral site: %.2f%%\n", prob_neutral * 100))
  cat("\n")
}

Now that we can use the log odds of winning for each time and compute their percentage chance of doing so, we are ready to simulate the playoffs. First we need to create the functions that match each team to their BT Model log odds estimate and then exponentiate them. Next we create a function that simulates two teams with their percentage chance of winnings against each other, one at home, and the other away, and simulates a winner. This is where our random sampling happens for our upcoming Monte Carlo simulation we run. It selects a random number and lines that number up with the win probabilities to determine a winner.

Our next function simulates a full series, with options for every best of format (3, 5, & 7) and codes which team is the home and which is the away in each game of the series. This is important as it ensures that the odds for each game are matched with which team is home and away in that game. As mentioned in the beginning, each different series length has different home and away game structures, and it’s crucial we don’t give home team advantage to the team that should be on the road in that game as it would completely change the odds of that team winning the game and series as a whole.

Once that is assured, we create the league championship simulation function for the first two rounds that implements when to use the Best of 3, 5 and 7 series rules and adding in our re-arranged match-ups after the Wild-Card Round. This function also crucially details which teams get home field advantage in each series at each level.

bt_prob <- function(home_team, away_team, i_is_home = TRUE) {
  coefs <- coef(fit)
  home_coef_name <- paste0("team", home_team)
  away_coef_name <- paste0("team", away_team)
  home_ability <- ifelse(home_coef_name %in% names(coefs), coefs[home_coef_name], 0)
  away_ability <- ifelse(away_coef_name %in% names(coefs), coefs[away_coef_name], 0)
  log_odds <- (home_ability - away_ability)
  if(i_is_home) {
    home_adv <- coefs["at.home"]
    if(!is.na(home_adv)) {
      log_odds <- log_odds + home_adv
    }
  }
  prob <- exp(log_odds) / (1 + exp(log_odds))
  return(prob)
}

sim_game <- function(home_team, away_team){
  runif(1) < bt_prob(home_team, away_team, i_is_home = TRUE)
}

sim_series <- function(high_seed, low_seed, type = c("BO3","BO5","BO7")){
  type <- match.arg(type)
  pattern <- switch(type,
                    BO3 = c("H","H","H"),
                    BO5 = c("H","H","A","A","H"),
                    BO7 = c("H","H","A","A","H","A","H"))
  hs_w <- 0; ls_w <- 0
  need <- if (type=="BO3") 2 else if (type=="BO5") 3 else 4

  for(g in seq_along(pattern)){
    home <- if (pattern[g]=="H") high_seed else low_seed
    away <- if (pattern[g]=="H") low_seed  else high_seed
    if (sim_game(home, away)) {
      if (home==high_seed) hs_w <- hs_w + 1 else ls_w <- ls_w + 1
    } else {
      if (away==high_seed) hs_w <- hs_w + 1 else ls_w <- ls_w + 1
    }
    if (hs_w==need) return(high_seed)
    if (ls_w==need) return(low_seed)
  }
  if (hs_w >= ls_w) high_seed else low_seed
}

sim_league_champ <- function(seeds_tbl){
  t1 <- seeds_tbl$team_key[seeds_tbl$seed==1]
  t2 <- seeds_tbl$team_key[seeds_tbl$seed==2]
  t3 <- seeds_tbl$team_key[seeds_tbl$seed==3]
  t4 <- seeds_tbl$team_key[seeds_tbl$seed==4]
  t5 <- seeds_tbl$team_key[seeds_tbl$seed==5]
  t6 <- seeds_tbl$team_key[seeds_tbl$seed==6]

  # Wild Card (BO3)
  wc1_winner <- sim_series(t4, t5, "BO3")
  wc2_winner <- sim_series(t3, t6, "BO3")
  
  # Determine which WC winner is lower seed for reseeding
  seed_of <- function(team_key) seeds_tbl$seed[match(team_key, seeds_tbl$team_key)]
  
  # Reseed: lowest remaining seed plays #1
  wc_winners <- c(wc1_winner, wc2_winner)
  wc_seeds <- sapply(wc_winners, seed_of)
  
  lower_seed_winner <- wc_winners[which.max(wc_seeds)]  # Higher seed number = lower seed
  higher_seed_winner <- wc_winners[which.min(wc_seeds)] # Lower seed number = higher seed

  # LDS (BO5) with reseeding
  d1_winner <- sim_series(t1, lower_seed_winner, "BO5")  # #1 vs lowest remaining
  d2_winner <- sim_series(t2, higher_seed_winner, "BO5") # #2 vs other WC winner

  # LCS (BO7) - higher seed gets home advantage
  lcs_seeds <- sapply(c(d1_winner, d2_winner), seed_of)
  high <- if (lcs_seeds[1] < lcs_seeds[2]) d1_winner else d2_winner
  low  <- if (identical(high, d1_winner)) d2_winner else d1_winner
  lcs_winner <- sim_series(high, low, "BO7")
  
  return(list(
    wild_card = list(
      matchup1 = list(team1 = t4, team2 = t5, winner = wc1_winner),
      matchup2 = list(team1 = t3, team2 = t6, winner = wc2_winner)
    ),
    lds = list(
      matchup1 = list(team1 = t1, team2 = lower_seed_winner, winner = d1_winner),
      matchup2 = list(team1 = t2, team2 = higher_seed_winner, winner = d2_winner)
    ),
    lcs = list(
      team1 = high, team2 = low, winner = lcs_winner
    ),
    champion = lcs_winner
  ))
}

simulate_playoffs <- function(N_SIM = 2000){
  champs <- character(N_SIM)
  al_ch <- character(N_SIM)
  nl_ch <- character(N_SIM)
  al_details <- list()
  nl_details <- list()
  
  for(s in seq_len(N_SIM)){
    al_details[[s]] <- sim_league_champ(seeds_AL)
    nl_details[[s]] <- sim_league_champ(seeds_NL)
    al_ch[s] <- al_details[[s]]$champion
    nl_ch[s] <- nl_details[[s]]$champion
    seed_of_AL <- seeds_AL$seed[match(al_ch[s], seeds_AL$team_key)]
    seed_of_NL <- seeds_NL$seed[match(nl_ch[s], seeds_NL$team_key)]

    if (seed_of_AL < seed_of_NL) {
      champs[s] <- sim_series(al_ch[s], nl_ch[s], "BO7")
    } else if (seed_of_NL < seed_of_AL) {
      champs[s] <- sim_series(nl_ch[s], al_ch[s], "BO7")
    } else {
      arow <- standings %>% filter(team_key==al_ch[s])
      nrow <- standings %>% filter(team_key==nl_ch[s])
      al_better <- (arow$Wpct > nrow$Wpct) | ((arow$Wpct == nrow$Wpct) & (arow$W > nrow$W))
      champs[s] <- if (isTRUE(al_better)) sim_series(al_ch[s], nl_ch[s], "BO7") else sim_series(nl_ch[s], al_ch[s], "BO7")
    }
  }
  return(list(
    ws = tibble(team_key = champs) %>% count(team_key, name="WS_titles") %>%
      mutate(pct = WS_titles/sum(WS_titles)) %>%
      left_join(team_map %>% select(team_key, team), by="team_key") %>%
      arrange(desc(pct)),
    al = tibble(team_key = al_ch) %>% count(team_key, name="AL_titles") %>%
      mutate(pct = AL_titles/sum(AL_titles)) %>%
      left_join(team_map %>% select(team_key, team), by="team_key") %>%
      arrange(desc(pct)),
    nl = tibble(team_key = nl_ch) %>% count(team_key, name="NL_titles") %>%
      mutate(pct = NL_titles/sum(NL_titles)) %>%
      left_join(team_map %>% select(team_key, team), by="team_key") %>%
      arrange(desc(pct)),
    al_details = al_details,
    nl_details = nl_details
  ))
}

calculate_round_probs <- function(details, seeds_tbl, league_name) {
  teams <- seeds_tbl$team_key
  
  wc1_winners <- sapply(details, function(x) x$wild_card$matchup1$winner)
  wc2_winners <- sapply(details, function(x) x$wild_card$matchup2$winner)
  lds1_winners <- sapply(details, function(x) x$lds$matchup1$winner)
  lds2_winners <- sapply(details, function(x) x$lds$matchup2$winner)
  lcs_winners <- sapply(details, function(x) x$lcs$winner)
  
  matchup_probs <- bind_rows(
    # Wild Card
    data.frame(team = teams) %>%
      mutate(wc1_prob = sapply(teams, function(t) mean(wc1_winners == t)),
             wc2_prob = sapply(teams, function(t) mean(wc2_winners == t))) %>%
      pivot_longer(cols = c(wc1_prob, wc2_prob), names_to = "round", values_to = "probability") %>%
      mutate(round = ifelse(round == "wc1_prob", "wild_card1", "wild_card2")),
    
    # LDS
    data.frame(team = teams) %>%
      mutate(lds1_prob = sapply(teams, function(t) mean(lds1_winners == t)),
             lds2_prob = sapply(teams, function(t) mean(lds2_winners == t))) %>%
      pivot_longer(cols = c(lds1_prob, lds2_prob), names_to = "round", values_to = "probability") %>%
      mutate(round = ifelse(round == "lds1_prob", "lds1", "lds2")),
    
    # LCS
    data.frame(team = teams, probability = sapply(teams, function(t) mean(lcs_winners == t))) %>%
      mutate(round = "lcs")
  ) %>% filter(probability > 0)
  
  total_advancement <- bind_rows(
    # Wild Card to LDS
    data.frame(
      team = teams,
      probability = sapply(teams, function(t) {
        mean(sapply(details, function(sim) {
          t %in% c(sim$wild_card$matchup1$winner, sim$wild_card$matchup2$winner)
        }))
      }),
      round = "advance_to_lds"
    ),
    # LDS to LCS
    data.frame(
      team = teams,
      probability = sapply(teams, function(t) {
        mean(sapply(details, function(sim) {
          t %in% c(sim$lds$matchup1$winner, sim$lds$matchup2$winner)
        }))
      }),
      round = "advance_to_lcs"
    ),
    # LCS to WS
    data.frame(
      team = teams,
      probability = sapply(teams, function(t) mean(lcs_winners == t)),
      round = "advance_to_ws"
    )
  ) %>% filter(probability > 0)
  list(
    matchup_odds = matchup_probs %>% arrange(round, desc(probability)),
    advancement_odds = total_advancement %>% arrange(round, desc(probability))
  )
}

To wrap up, it is clear and obvious based on our 2000 simulations of our model that by re-seeding the Playoffs by regular season record and restructuring the match-ups after the first round, the teams that earned a top two seed in their league were most likely to win the World Series. It wasn’t guaranteed, all 12 teams won the title at least 29 times and 7 of the 12 won it over 100 times, but it was much more likely these top 2 seeds from either league won the title, winning 1357 of the 2000 simulations, 67.85%. It was even between the top teams, a slight advantage to the 1 seeds as they won more often than the 2 seeds but that was more based on latent strength than playoff structure. Overall this model gave the best teams from the regular season the best chance to win the title, but no so heavily to remove the opportunity for a lower seed to win it all. If the MLB was looking to reformat their Playoff structure, based on our Bradley-Terry Model and Monte Carlo simulation, we believe this playoff format would fit the purpose of giving the best teams through 162 regular season games an earned advantage to win the title.
